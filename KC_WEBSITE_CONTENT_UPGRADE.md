### Evergy Net Metering — The Real Story (Missouri vs. Kansas)

Net metering is how Evergy gives solar customers credit for extra energy they produce.
The rules are different depending on which side of the state line you're on.

#### Missouri Side (Evergy Missouri Metro / Missouri West)

- Your solar system produces power during the day.
- What you use immediately counts against your bill at full retail rate.
- What you don't use immediately gets exported to the grid.
- Evergy credits you for that exported energy — at the **retail rate**.
- Those credits roll forward month to month within your annual billing cycle.
- At end of year, any remaining unused credits are paid out at a lower wholesale rate.
- **Bottom line for homeowners**: Size your system to roughly match your annual usage.
  You get full value for everything you produce as long as it offsets your own consumption.
- Rate increases in 2025 alone: Evergy Missouri West raised rates ~14% effective Jan 1, 2025.
  Evergy Missouri Metro has filed for a **14.9% additional increase** (2026 request).

#### Kansas Side (Evergy Kansas Metro / Kansas Central)

- Works similarly to Missouri with one critical difference: **don't oversize.**
- Excess energy exported beyond what you consume is credited at **wholesale rate
  (~2.4¢/kWh)** — not retail (~13–14¢/kWh).
- You would be paid pennies for energy Evergy then sells to your neighbors at retail.
- **New rule as of January 1, 2026**: New net metering customers in Kansas are limited
  to exporting no more than 50% of their system's export capacity.
  This effectively makes oversizing economically pointless.
- Kansas rates increased ~9.6% (base rate) in 2025, adding ~$8.47/month for average customer.
- **Bottom line for Kansas homeowners**: Right-size your system to your consumption.
  A well-sized system in Kansas still dramatically reduces your bill.
  An oversized one just subsidizes Evergy.

#### Simple Explanation for Homeowners (use this language):

> "Evergy tracks what your solar produces and what you use. When your panels are running,
> your meter runs backward. At night you pull from the grid as normal. Most months
> your bill drops significantly — or hits near zero. We size your system specifically
> to your usage so you're not giving energy away at a loss."

**Q: Does Evergy allow solar panels on my home?**

Yes — Evergy is required by state law to offer net metering to residential solar customers
in both Kansas and Missouri. They have a formal interconnection process that we handle
on your behalf. You don't need to negotiate with them or navigate their paperwork.

---

**Q: How expensive is solar?**

Installed system costs in Kansas City typically run $18,000–$26,000 depending on
your home's size and energy usage. With $0 down financing, most homeowners pay
$130–$160/month — which is less than their current Evergy bill. You're not spending
more. You're redirecting what you already pay.

If your bill is under $75/month, we'll tell you honestly that solar probably won't
pencil out. We'd rather lose a small deal than send you into something that doesn't work.

---

**Q: What happens to extra solar energy I produce?**

Evergy credits your account for the extra energy at the retail rate (Missouri) or
applies it to reduce your bill (Kansas). On sunny spring and fall days when your panels
run hot, those credits roll forward. Most KC solar homeowners end up with near-zero
bills several months per year.

One important note for Kansas homeowners: we design your system to match your usage —
not overproduce — because excess power sent back to Evergy past your annual usage
is credited at wholesale rates, not retail. Right-sizing protects your investment.

---

**Q: Is solar still worth it without the tax credit?**

Yes — and here's why the math still works.

The 30% federal tax credit expired at the end of 2025. But the economics of solar
aren't built on that credit — they're built on the difference between what you pay
Evergy now versus what a fixed solar payment costs.

If you pay $180/month to Evergy today, and Evergy raises rates 10–14% (which they
have been doing), you'll be paying $200+ within a year or two. Your solar payment
stays the same. That gap compounds every year for 25 years.

The payback timeline is longer without the credit — typically 8–12 years on an owned
system. But with panels warrantied for 25 years and Evergy rates climbing, the
long-term savings are still very real.

---

**Q: What if I sell my house?**

Solar increases your home's appraised value — typically by 3–4% according to national
studies. A $250,000 home with solar can appraise $7,500–$10,000 higher. Your financing
transfers to the buyer at closing or can be paid off from the sale. Either way, it's an
asset — not a liability.

---

**Q: How long does installation actually take?**

One day for the installation itself. The total process — permits, Evergy interconnection
approval, inspection — typically runs 3–4 weeks. We manage all of it. You don't have
to call Evergy, file paperwork, or chase anyone down. One point of contact from start
to live system.

## PART 6: WEAK SPOTS & RISKS

### 1. CRITICAL — Your installer rebate claim needs to be specific
The brief mentions "cash rebate (installer-specific program)" as a replacement for the tax credit. I could not confirm what KC Energy Advisors specifically offers. Before publishing, define this exactly:
- What is the rebate amount?
- Who qualifies?
- Is it a financing rate, a cash-back amount, or a bill credit?
Do NOT publish a rebate claim without a specific, defensible number.

### 2. Verify the 14.9% rate increase status
Evergy filed for a 14.9% increase for Missouri Metro in January 2026. As of this writing,
it may still be pending regulatory approval. Confirm current status before publishing.
Say "Evergy has filed for another increase" (true regardless of approval status) rather
than "Evergy raised rates 14.9%" (only true if approved).

### 3. Kansas net metering credits at retail vs. wholesale — get precise
Current Kansas net metering law credits at retail within the billing period. Year-end
surplus is paid at wholesale (~2.4¢). Confirm with your team that this matches what
you're seeing in practice — KCC rules updated in 2024 (HB 2527) and interpretations
can vary by service territory.

### 4. Be careful with payback period claims
"8–12 years" for an owned system (no tax credit) is conservative and defensible.
Don't publish shorter break-even claims without running the specific math.
Let Michael run personalized numbers rather than publishing a single figure.

### 5. "60–90% bill reduction" — confirm your typical results
This is a wide range. If your installed systems consistently hit 80–90%, narrow it.
If you're seeing 50–70% for some homeowners, say so. Specific is better than optimistic.
